<p className="text-lg leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">"How fast is Solana?" is one of the most-searched questions about the network, and one of the most poorly answered. Headline numbers range from a few thousand transactions per second to over a million, and they are all "true" in different senses. This guide separates real-world throughput from stress-test ceilings, explains the block-limit changes driving capacity in 2026, and clears up why Solana's TPS figures are so easy to misread.</p>

<div className="not-prose rounded-2xl border border-emerald-500/20 bg-emerald-500/5 p-5">
  <p className="text-xs font-semibold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-400">Quick answer</p>
  <p className="mt-2 text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">In 2026, Solana sustains <strong className="text-white">several thousand transactions per second</strong> in normal production, up from roughly 1,700 per second in mid-2025. Stress tests have briefly passed <strong className="text-white">100,000 per second</strong>, and the Firedancer client has shown over <strong className="text-white">one million per second</strong> in testing. The gap exists because real throughput is limited by demand and protocol block limits, not by the software's ceiling.</p>

  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">

  <div className="mt-6 space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>The headline million-per-second figure is a hardware-and-software capability, not a description of typical mainnet traffic. Real throughput is constrained by how many transactions users actually submit and by the protocol's block limits, both of which sit far below the client's raw capacity. A useful real-world data point: during the large market sell-off in October 2025, the network sustained very high packet rates and full blocks without service degradation, which is exactly the kind of stress that used to break it.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="compute-units" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">What is a compute unit, and what is the block limit?</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>Solana meters work in compute units (CUs), roughly analogous to Ethereum's gas. Every transaction requests a compute-unit limit up front, and each block has a maximum total compute budget. Block limits exist primarily to ensure the vast majority of validators can keep pace with block production: they cap how much work a leader is allowed to pack into a block.</p>
    <p>The block limit has been rising. In mid-2025, <a href="https://github.com/solana-foundation/solana-improvement-documents" className="text-emerald-400 underline-offset-4 transition hover:text-emerald-300 hover:underline">SIMD-0256</a> raised the per-block ceiling to 60 million compute units, which at the time helped the network process around 1,700 transactions per second during daytime traffic. Each block spans roughly 400 milliseconds, so this budget is consumed and refreshed several times per second.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="proposals" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">SIMD-0286 and SIMD-0370: where block capacity is heading</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>Two proposals define the next stage of Solana's capacity story.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="not-prose mt-6 grid gap-4 md:grid-cols-2">
    <div className="rounded-2xl border border-emerald-500/20 bg-emerald-500/5 p-5">
      <p className="text-xs font-semibold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-400">SIMD-0286</p>
      <p className="mt-2 font-semibold text-white">Raise the block limit to 100M CU</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">Proposes raising the per-block limit from 60 million to 100 million compute units, a 66% increase, authored by Jito Labs' Lucas Bruder. The motivation is that recent mainnet performance suggests blocks are no longer constrained by execution time, so heavy applications such as order-book exchanges and complex multi-step DeFi transactions can be given more room before they hit "compute budget exceeded" errors. Crucially, it raises only the total block limit; the per-account write limit (roughly 12 million compute units) stays the same, so the extra capacity benefits parallelizable transactions like swaps and mints rather than adding pressure on any single account.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="rounded-2xl border border-emerald-500/20 bg-emerald-500/5 p-5">
      <p className="text-xs font-semibold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-400">SIMD-0370</p>
      <p className="mt-2 font-semibold text-white">Remove the fixed block limit entirely</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">From the Firedancer team at Jump Crypto, goes further: it proposes removing the fixed block limit entirely so that block size scales dynamically, bounded only by the processing capability of the most performant validators. The idea is a "performance flywheel," where well-resourced block producers upgrade hardware to pack more transactions and earn more rewards, lifting overall network capacity. This proposal is positioned to build on the foundational improvements from the <a href="/blog/what-is-alpenglow" className="text-emerald-400 underline-offset-4 transition hover:text-emerald-300 hover:underline">Alpenglow upgrade</a>.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="not-prose mt-6 rounded-xl border-l-4 border-amber-500 bg-zinc-900/40 p-4">
    <p className="text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-300"><strong className="text-white">A real debate, not just hype:</strong> dynamic or larger blocks raise legitimate centralization concerns. If keeping up requires ever more expensive hardware, smaller validators could be squeezed out, concentrating stake among a few powerful operators. This trade-off between raw throughput and decentralization is actively contested in Solana governance.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="misread" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">Why Solana's TPS numbers are so easy to misread</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>Here is the nuance that almost every "Solana does X TPS" claim ignores: historically, a large majority of Solana's transactions were validator votes, not user activity. Votes have made up roughly three-quarters of transactions, which means raw TPS figures substantially overstate genuine user throughput.</p>
    <p>This matters enormously for 2026, because the <a href="/blog/what-is-alpenglow" className="text-emerald-400 underline-offset-4 transition hover:text-emerald-300 hover:underline">Alpenglow upgrade</a> moves validator votes off-chain. Once that happens, vote transactions stop appearing in the count, so the raw TPS number will drop even as usable capacity for real transactions rises. In other words, headline TPS before and after Alpenglow will not be comparing the same thing. When you read a Solana throughput statistic, always ask whether it includes vote transactions.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="not-prose mt-6 rounded-xl border-l-4 border-emerald-500 bg-zinc-900/40 p-4">
    <p className="text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-300"><strong className="text-white">The one question to ask:</strong> does this TPS figure include validator votes? Before Alpenglow, roughly three-quarters of transactions were votes. After it, they vanish from the count, so a lower headline number can actually mean more real user capacity.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="client-protocol" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">How the client and the protocol interact</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>It is worth being clear about what limits throughput today. The <a href="/blog/what-is-firedancer" className="text-emerald-400 underline-offset-4 transition hover:text-emerald-300 hover:underline">Firedancer client</a> can already process far more than current block limits permit, so in 2026 the binding constraint is increasingly the protocol's block limits and validator hardware, not the client software. That is precisely why the block-limit proposals above exist: the software outran the protocol's caps.</p>
    <p>Beyond block limits, the network's longer-term scaling work targets the physical layer too. An initiative often summarized as "increase bandwidth, reduce latency" underpins much of this roadmap, and a dedicated low-latency networking layer for validators (delivering block data over private fiber) has been rolling out to improve propagation. The Solana Foundation has tied these efforts to an explicit ambition to support internet-scale capital markets later this decade.</p>
  </div>
</section>
