<p className="text-lg leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">Alpenglow is the most ambitious change to Solana's core design since the network launched. It rewrites how validators agree on the state of the chain, cutting deterministic finality by roughly two orders of magnitude and removing the vote transactions that currently fill most of every block. This guide explains what Alpenglow is, how Votor works, the economics around it, and exactly where it stands on the road to mainnet.</p>

<div className="not-prose rounded-2xl border border-emerald-500/20 bg-emerald-500/5 p-5">
  <p className="text-xs font-semibold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-400">Quick answer</p>
  <p className="mt-2 text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-300"><strong className="text-white">Alpenglow</strong> (SIMD-0326) is Solana's consensus rewrite. It replaces Proof of History and Tower BFT with a direct-voting protocol called <strong className="text-white">Votor</strong>, reducing deterministic finality from about 12.8 seconds to roughly 100 to 150 ms and moving validator votes off-chain so they no longer consume block space. It passed governance in September 2025 and is expected on mainnet later in 2026.</p>

<section id="problem" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">What problem does Alpenglow solve?</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>To understand Alpenglow, you need to know how Solana confirms transactions today. The current system combines Proof of History (a cryptographic clock) with Tower BFT (a Byzantine-fault-tolerant voting protocol). Under this design a transaction reaches an early "optimistic" confirmation within a few hundred milliseconds, but full, irreversible finality takes roughly 12.8 seconds.</p>
    <p>That gap creates two problems. First, the long road to finality slows down any application that needs certainty, from cross-border settlement to high-frequency trading. Second, the voting process itself is expensive: validators broadcast their votes as on-chain transactions, and those vote transactions consume around three-quarters of all block space. Alpenglow tackles both at once.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="changes" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">What exactly does Alpenglow change?</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>Alpenglow, formalized as SIMD-0326, replaces the consensus layer with two new components and a different notion of time.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="not-prose mt-6 grid gap-4 md:grid-cols-3">
    <div className="rounded-2xl border border-zinc-800 bg-zinc-900/40 p-5">
      <p className="text-xs font-semibold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-400">Component</p>
      <p className="mt-2 font-semibold text-white">Votor</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-400">A lightweight, direct-vote finality protocol that collapses Tower BFT's lengthy, multi-step confirmation into one or two quick voting rounds.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="rounded-2xl border border-zinc-800 bg-zinc-900/40 p-5">
      <p className="text-xs font-semibold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-400">Component</p>
      <p className="mt-2 font-semibold text-white">Rotor</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-400">A new block-propagation layer intended to eventually replace Turbine. Rotor is deferred to its own separate proposal; at activation, Turbine stays in place, so day-one breaking changes are confined to consensus.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="rounded-2xl border border-zinc-800 bg-zinc-900/40 p-5">
      <p className="text-xs font-semibold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-400">Component</p>
      <p className="mt-2 font-semibold text-white">A new sense of time</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-400">Proof of History is removed as a consensus mechanism and replaced by a fixed roughly 400 ms block time plus local timeout timers on each validator. The 400 ms slot cadence itself does not change.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <p className="mt-6 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">The single biggest user-facing effect is speed: finality drops from about 12.8 seconds to a target of 100 to 150 ms.</p>
</section>

<section id="votor" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">How does Votor reach finality so quickly?</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>Votor uses two paths depending on network conditions.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="not-prose mt-6 overflow-x-auto rounded-2xl border border-zinc-800">
    <table className="w-full border-collapse text-left text-sm">
      <thead className="bg-zinc-900/60">
        <tr>
          <th className="px-4 py-3 font-semibold text-emerald-400">Path</th>
          <th className="px-4 py-3 font-semibold text-emerald-400">Finality time</th>
          <th className="px-4 py-3 font-semibold text-emerald-400">Requirement</th>
        </tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody className="text-zinc-300">
        <tr className="border-t border-zinc-800">
          <td className="px-4 py-3 font-medium text-white">Fast path</td>
          <td className="px-4 py-3">~100 ms</td>
          <td className="px-4 py-3">At least 80% of stake agrees in a single round</td>
        </tr>
        <tr className="border-t border-zinc-800">
          <td className="px-4 py-3 font-medium text-white">Slow path</td>
          <td className="px-4 py-3">~150 ms</td>
          <td className="px-4 py-3">At least 60% agreement across two rounds</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </div>

  <div className="mt-6 space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>Instead of posting votes as on-chain transactions, validators exchange votes directly with one another. Once a block leader has collected enough votes, it aggregates the signatures using BLS signature aggregation, which compresses hundreds or thousands of signatures into a single compact "finality certificate." Only that certificate is published on-chain as proof. This is what frees up the roughly three-quarters of block space that vote transactions used to occupy.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="not-prose mt-6 rounded-xl border-l-4 border-emerald-500 bg-zinc-900/40 p-4">
    <p className="text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-300"><strong className="text-white">In plain terms:</strong> today, every vote is a letter mailed through the post office (the chain). Alpenglow lets validators talk directly, then mails a single signed summary instead of thousands of separate letters.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="security" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">Is Alpenglow more secure than the old design?</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>That is part of the motivation. Alpenglow comes with a formal academic analysis and a clearer resilience model, often summarized as "20 plus 20": the protocol is designed to remain safe with under 20% of stake acting maliciously, and under stronger assumptions can keep making progress even if a further roughly 20% of validators are simply offline.</p>
    <p>The work comes from Anza's research division, led by Professor Roger Wattenhofer of ETH Zurich, alongside Quentin Kniep and Kobi Sliwinski. Notably, Wattenhofer co-authored a 2024 paper that identified liveness weaknesses in Solana's existing consensus, so Alpenglow is in part a direct response to known shortcomings of the old design. The full technical details are in the <a href="https://www.anza.xyz/alpenglow-1-1" className="text-emerald-400 underline-offset-4 transition hover:text-emerald-300 hover:underline">Alpenglow White Paper v1.1</a>.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="economics" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">What are the economic changes around Alpenglow?</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>Removing on-chain votes changes validator economics, and a set of companion proposals handle the details. Today validators pay a small fee for every vote transaction, which adds up to roughly two SOL per epoch for an active validator. Once votes move off-chain, that cost disappears.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="not-prose mt-6 grid gap-4 md:grid-cols-2">
    <div className="rounded-2xl border border-zinc-800 bg-zinc-900/40 p-5">
      <p className="font-semibold text-white">Validator Admission Ticket (VAT)</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-400">A separate proposal introduces a fixed fee charged per epoch to validators eligible for the next epoch's consensus set, in the region of 1.6 SOL, which would be burned.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="rounded-2xl border border-zinc-800 bg-zinc-900/40 p-5">
      <p className="font-semibold text-white">BLS key management (SIMD-0387)</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm leading-relaxed text-zinc-400">A prerequisite proposal covers BLS keys. After activation, a validator that has not registered a BLS key is treated as unstaked: still online, but not counted in consensus and not earning rewards.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <p className="mt-6 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">The core SIMD-0326 keeps existing vote-reward mechanics intact while removing some harmful incentives (such as the incentive to delay a vote). Broader economic redesign is left to future, dedicated proposals.</p>
</section>

<section id="connect" className="scroll-mt-24">
  <h2 className="mb-6 text-3xl font-bold text-white md:text-4xl">How does Alpenglow connect to the other 2026 upgrades?</h2>
  <div className="space-y-4 leading-relaxed text-zinc-300">
    <p>Alpenglow is the consensus piece of a larger picture. By freeing the block space that vote transactions consumed, it directly complements <a href="/blog/solana-throughput-tps-2026" className="text-emerald-400 underline-offset-4 transition hover:text-emerald-300 hover:underline">Solana's throughput and block-limit changes</a>, since more usable space means more room for real transactions. It also eases <a href="/blog/solana-transaction-fees-local-fee-markets" className="text-emerald-400 underline-offset-4 transition hover:text-emerald-300 hover:underline">fee pressure during congestion</a> for the same reason. And because Votor is a simpler protocol than Tower BFT, it is easier to reimplement in a second client, which helps <a href="/blog/what-is-firedancer" className="text-emerald-400 underline-offset-4 transition hover:text-emerald-300 hover:underline">Firedancer</a> reach the multi-client parity Solana needs.</p>
  </div>
</section>
